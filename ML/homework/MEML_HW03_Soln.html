Problem 1

Find solution $D$ of the optimization problem with the following objective function and constraints. Here, $\lVert \cdot \rVert_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm (or distance).

(Hint: you do not have to find the optimal value of $Z$)



$$\begin{align*} D= \min_Z \;\; & \lVert X_1 - Z \rVert_2 + \lVert X_2 - Z \rVert_2 \\ \\ \text{subject to} \; & Z \in \text{span} \{V_1, V_2\} \end{align*}$$



Figure 2

Solution 1

$D^*$ is the shortest distance from $X_1$ to $X_2$ via $Z$ on the spanned hyperplane. The shortest line is the straight line from $X_1$ and $Y$ which is mirrored reflection of $X_2$ with respect to the hyperplane.


$$ \begin{align*} D^* &=\lVert MX_1 - X_2\rVert\\ &= \lVert X_1 - MX_2 \rVert \\ & = \lVert X_1 - \left( 2V \left(V^TV \right)^{-1}V^T-I\right)X_2\rVert \end{align*} $$

where $M$ is mirror transformation and $V = [V_1 \; V_2]$

Let $$ \begin{align*} f &= (2x_1 - 1)^2 + (-x_1 + x_2)^2 + (2x_2 +1)^2 \\ \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} &= 2(2x_1 - 1)2 + (-x_1 + x_2)(-1) = 10x_1 - 2x_2 - 4 = 0 \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} &= 2(-x_1 + x_2) + 2(2x_2 + 1)2 = -2x_1 + 10x_2 + 4 = 0 \\ \end{align*} $$


$$ \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} 10 & -2 \\ -2 & 10 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} &= 0 \\\\ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} 10 & -2 \\ -2 & -10 \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} \\\\ &= \frac{1}{96}\begin{bmatrix} 10 & 2 \\ 2 & 10 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} =\frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} $$


$$ \text{Thus} \ x_1 = \frac{1}{3} \quad \text{and}\quad x_2 = -\frac{1}{3} $$

(c)

Zero (or one global optimum). This function has no subpotimal point since it is convex. We need to prove the function is a convex function


$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} =2(2x_1 - 1)2 + (-x_1 + x_2)(-1) = \ 10x_1 - 2x_2 - 4 = 0 $$$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} =2(-x_1 + x_2) + 2(2x_2 + 1)2 = \ -2x_1 + 10x_2 + 4 = 0 $$



$$ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} =2\cdot2\cdot2 + 2 = 10 \qquad \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_2} = -2 $$

$$ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} =2 + 2\cdot2\cdot2 = 10 \qquad \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_2} = -2 $$

$f(x) = \mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}$ & it is twice differentiable over an open domain.

Then,

$$ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}= \begin{bmatrix}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2}& \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_2} \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_2}& \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix}10&-2 \\ -2&10 \end{bmatrix}\ $$


Use Hessian determinant

$$\det \left(\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} \right)= 100 - 4 = 96 \geq 0 \quad \text{for all }x \in \text{domain}(f(x))$$

Thus, $f(x)$ is a convex function
