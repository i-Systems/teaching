Problem 1

(a) [3pt] Find the Hessian of the cost function


$$J(\theta)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^m \left(\theta^{T}x^{(i)}-y^{(i)} \right)^{2} = \frac{1}{2} \lVert X\theta - y\rVert ^2$$$$\text{Hessian} = \frac{\partial^2J(\theta)}{\partial \theta_j \partial \theta_k} = H_{jk}$$


Hint) the Hessian matrix or Hessian is a square matrix of second-order partial derivatives of a scalar-valued function.


(b) [5pt] Prove that first iteration of the Newton's method gives us the solution of the least squares problem, $ \theta = (X^{T}X)^{-1}X^{T}{y}$


Hint) Newton's Method

Let $f: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable function. We seek a solution of $f(\theta) = 0$, starting from an initial estimate $\theta=\theta^{(0)}$. At the $n$'th step, given $\theta^{(n)}$, compute the next approximation $\theta^{(n+1)}$ by

$$\theta^{(n+1)} = \theta^{(n)} - \frac{f\left(\theta^{(n)} \right)}{f'\left(\theta^{(n)}\right)} \quad \text{ and repeat}$$

Hint) Matrix derivatives


(c) [3pt] Write down the difference between the gradient descent method and the Newton's method when it comes to solving the least squares problem.


Solution 1

(a) [3pt] Find the Hessian of the cost function


$$J(\theta)=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^m \left(\theta^{T}x^{(i)}-y^{(i)} \right)^{2} = \frac{1}{2} \lVert X\theta - y\rVert ^2$$$$\text{Hessian} = \frac{\partial^2J(\theta)}{\partial \theta_j \partial \theta_k} = H_{jk}$$


$$\begin{align*} \nabla J &= \frac{1}{2} \left(X^TX\theta + (X^TX)^T\theta - X^Ty - (y^TX)^T \right) = X^TX\theta - X^Ty \\ H & = X^TX \end{align*} $$


(b) [5pt] Prove that first iteration of the Newton's method gives us the solution of the least squares problem, $ \theta = (X^{T}X)^{-1}X^{T}{y}$


$$\theta^{(1)} = \theta^{(0)} - H^{-1}\nabla J(\theta^{(0)}) = \theta^{(0)} - (X^TX)^{-1} \left( X^TX\theta^{(0)} - X^Ty \right) = (X^{T}X)^{-1}X^{T}y$$


(c) [3pt] Write down the difference between the gradient descent method and the Newton's method when it comes to solving the least squares problem.

  • Newton's method has stronger constraints in terms of the differentiability of the function than gradient descent. If the second derivative of the function is undefined in the function's root, then we can apply gradient descent on it but not Newton's method.

Problem 2

In this problem we use least-squares to fit a circle to given points $(u_i, v_i)$ in a plane, as shown in the figure.




We use $(u_c, v_c)$ to denote the center of the circle and $R$ for its radius. A point $(u,v)$ is on the circle if $(u-u_c)^2 + (v-v_c)^2 = R^2$. We can therefore formulate the fitting problem as


$$\text{minimize } \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left( (u_i-u_c)^2 + (v_i-v_c)^2 - R^2 \right)^2$$

With variables $v_c, u_c$, and $R$.


(a) [3pt] Show that this can be written as a linear least-squares problem if we make a change of variables and use as variables $u_c, v_c$ and $\omega = u_c^2 + v_c^2 -R^2$.


(b) [3pt] Find $A, b$, and $x$ in the equivalent linear least-squares formulations (i.e., $ \min \; \rVert Ax - b \lVert^2_2$).


Solution 2

Let $$ \begin{align*} f = (u_i-u_c)^2 + (v_i-v_c)^2 - R^2\\ \end{align*} $$


So we can convert $f$ like this,


$$ \begin{align*} f &=u_i^2 - 2u_iu_c + u_c^2 + v_i^2 - 2v_iv_c + v_c^2 - R^2\\ &= (u_i^2 + v_i^2) + (-2u_iu_c - 2v_iv_c) + (u_c^2 + v_c^2 - R^2) \\ & = (u_i^2 + v_i^2) + (-2u_i u_c - 2v_i v_c) + \omega \\ & = -2u_i u_c - 2v_i v_c + \omega + u_i^2 + v_i^2 \end{align*} $$


Consequently, changing the situation in question to a matrix form would result in the following:



$$ \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} -2u_1 & -2v_1 & 1\\ -2u_2 & -2v_2 & 1\\ \vdots &\vdots &\vdots \\ -2u_m & -2v_m & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} u_c\\ v_c\\ \omega\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -u_1^2 - v_1^2\\ -u_2^2 - v_2^2\\ \vdots \\ -u_m^2 - v_2^m \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} $$


Since this becomes a linear form of $Ax = b$, and $A$ becomes a matrix of skinny shapes of $m\times3$, the method of last-square should be used to obtain the optimal $x^*$ of $\min \; \lVert Ax - b\rVert_2^2$.

Problem 3

Consider the elastic-net regression problem:


$$\min \rVert y - A \theta \lVert_2^2 + \lambda \left[ \alpha \rVert \theta \lVert^2_2 + (1-\alpha)\rVert \theta \lVert_1 \right]$$


[6pt] Show how one can turn this into a LASSO problem, using an augmented version of $A$ and $y$

(i.e., Find $y', A'$, and $\lambda'$ in the equivalently transformed LASSO, $\min \; \lVert y^\prime - A^\prime \theta \rVert_2^2 + \lambda^\prime \lVert \theta \rVert_1$).


Hint) Elastic-net is a popular type of regularized linear regression that combines two popular penalties, specifically the L1 and L2 penalty functions.

Solution 3

Consider the elastic-net regression problem:


$$\min \rVert y - A \theta \lVert_2^2 + \lambda \left[ \alpha \rVert \theta \lVert^2_2 + (1-\alpha)\rVert \theta \lVert_1 \right]$$


[5pt] Show how one can turn this into a Lasso problem, using an augmented version of $A$ and $y$.


$$\begin{align*} \mathcal{L} &= \min \lVert y - A \theta \rVert_2^2 + \lambda \left[ \alpha \rVert \theta \lVert^2_2 + (1-\alpha)\rVert \theta \lVert_1 \right]\\ & = \min \Bigg\Vert \begin{bmatrix} y\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} A\\ \sqrt{\lambda \alpha} \end{bmatrix} \Bigg\Vert^2_{2} + \lambda \left(1 - \alpha \right) \lVert \theta \rVert_1 \\ \end{align*}$$


$$\begin{align*} &\begin{bmatrix} y\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = y^\prime, \quad \begin{bmatrix} A\\ \sqrt{\lambda \alpha} \end{bmatrix} = A^\prime, \quad \lambda \left(1 - \alpha \right) = \lambda^\prime \\ \end{align*}$$


$$\begin{align*} \therefore \mathcal{L} = \min \lVert y^\prime - A^\prime \theta \rVert_2^2 + \lambda^\prime \lVert \theta \rVert_1 \\ \end{align*}$$

Problem 4

(a) [3pt] John is going to classify images of dogs and cats using machine learning. However, it is difficult for him to define a loss. If he defines the loss into discrete output space: $\mathcal{L} = \{1,0\}$, is it possible to optimize this loss using the gradient descent method. And explain why.

Hint) Consider discrete loss functions such as a step function.

(b) [10pt] Describe the loss function and derive the gradient descent algorithm in the logistic regression.

Solution 4

(a) It is impossible to apply gradient descent algorithm to the discrete loss function. This is because the gradient is zero, which means that weights are not updated.

(b) [10pt] To use the gradient descent method, we need to find the derivative of it


$$\begin{align*} \nabla \ell \left(\omega \right) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial \ell \left(\omega \right)}{\partial \omega_1} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial \ell \left(\omega \right)}{\partial \omega_n} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}$$


We need to compute $\frac{\partial \ell \left(\omega \right)}{\partial \omega_j}$


$$\begin{align*} \ell \left(\omega \right) = log \mathcal{L} \left(\omega \right) = \sum_{i=1}^m y^{(i)} logh_w \left(x^{(i)} \right) + \left(1 - y^{(i)} \right)log \left(1 - h_{\omega} \left(x^{(i)} \right) \right) \end{align*}$$

Think about a single data point with a single parameter $\omega$ for the simplicity


$$\begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial \omega} \left[ylog(\sigma) + (1 - y)log(1 - \sigma) \right] & = y \frac{\sigma'}{\sigma} + (1 - y) \frac{-\sigma}{1 - \sigma} \\ & = \left(\frac{y}{\sigma} - \frac{1 - y}{1 - \sigma} \right)\sigma' \\ & = \frac{y - \sigma}{\sigma \left(1 - \sigma \right)}\sigma' \\ & = \frac{y - \sigma}{\sigma \left(1 - \sigma \right)}\sigma \left(1 - \sigma \right)x \\ & = \left(y - \sigma \right)x \end{align*}$$

For $m$ training data points with parameters $\omega$


$$\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \ell \left(\omega \right)}{\partial \omega_j} = \sum_{i=1}^m \left(y^{(i)} - h_{\omega} \left(x^{(i)} \right) \right)x_j^{(i)} \quad \overset{\underset{\mathrm{vectorization}}{}}{=} \quad \left(y - h_{\omega} \left(x \right) \right)^Tx_j = x_j^T \left(y - h_{\omega} \left(x \right) \right) \end{align*}$$


$$\begin{align*} \nabla \ell \left(\omega \right) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial \ell \left(\omega \right)}{\partial \omega_1} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial \ell \left(\omega \right)}{\partial \omega_n} \end{bmatrix} = X^T \left(y - h_{\omega} \left(x \right) \right) = X^T \left(y - \sigma \left(X \omega \right) \right) \end{align*}$$


$$\begin{align*} \therefore \quad \omega \leftarrow \omega - \eta \left(- \nabla \ell \left(\omega \right) \right) \end{align*}$$
